# Jones Citation Framework

Thomas W. Jones's documentation framework requires that every citation answer five questions as fully as possible. This framework applies to all genealogical source types.

## The Five Questions

### 1. WHO — Who created or authored the source?

The person, agency, or entity responsible for creating the record or work.

**Examples by source type:**
- Vital record: "Pennsylvania Department of Health"
- Census: "United States Bureau of the Census"
- Church record: "St. James Lutheran Church, Lancaster, Pennsylvania"
- Authored book: "Smith, John A."
- Newspaper: "Reading Times" (the paper itself for unsigned pieces; named reporter for bylined articles)
- Personal document: "Allen, Mary (Mrs. Jacob Allen)" for a diary or letter
- Database: "Ancestry.com Operations, Inc." (the corporate entity that created the database)
- Compiled work: Name the compiler, not the original record creator

### 2. WHAT — What is the source?

The title (for published works) or a clear description (for unpublished records, manuscripts, and documents without formal titles).

**Examples:**
- Published book: *Pennsylvania German Pioneers* (italicized)
- Vital record: "Birth certificates, 1906–1915"
- Census: "1870 U.S. census, population schedule"
- Deed: "Deed Book N, Lancaster County"
- Obituary: "'Obituary of John Smith,' Reading Times"
- Online database: "U.S., Find a Grave Index, 1600s–Current" (database title as displayed)
- Manuscript: "Account book, 1847–1863" (descriptive, since untitled)
- Church record: "Baptismal register, 1780–1820"

### 3. WHEN — When was the source or recorded event created?

Publication year for published works. Event or recording date for manuscripts and original records.

**Examples:**
- Published book: "(1934)" — year of publication
- Vital record: "3 March 1889" — date of the event recorded
- Census: "1 June 1870" — enumeration date (or just "1870" if exact date unknown)
- Newspaper: "15 April 1911" — date of publication
- Online database: "accessed 11 March 2026" — date of access for digital sources
- Deed: "recorded 14 September 1802" — recording date

### 4. WHERE IS — Where is the source held or published?

The repository, website, or publication information that allows someone to locate the source.

**Examples:**
- Archive: "Pennsylvania State Archives, Harrisburg, Pennsylvania"
- Library: "Lancaster County Historical Society, Lancaster, Pennsylvania"
- Website: "Ancestry.com (https://www.ancestry.com)"
- Publisher: "Baltimore, Maryland: Genealogical Publishing Company"
- Church: "held by St. James Lutheran Church, Lancaster, Pennsylvania"
- Courthouse: "Lancaster County Recorder of Deeds, Lancaster, Pennsylvania"
- FamilySearch: "FamilySearch (https://www.familysearch.org)"
- Personal possession: "privately held by [name], [city, state]"

### 5. WHERE IN — Where in the source is the specific information?

The precise location within the source where the information appears. This must be specific enough for someone to find the exact same data.

**Examples:**
- Book: "vol. 3, p. 214, entry 17"
- Census: "Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, Earl Township, p. 12, dwelling 87, family 91, line 22"
- Database: "database with images, entry for William Jones, household of Mary Jones"
- Microfilm: "FHL microfilm 1,234,567, item 3, image 42"
- Deed book: "Book N, p. 312"
- Certificate: "certificate no. 12345"
- Newspaper: "p. 4, col. 3"
- Digital image: "image 42 of 156"
- Church register: "p. 17, entry for baptism of Anna Erb, 12 May 1793"

## Citation Coaching Process

When helping a researcher cite a source, walk them through each question in order:

1. Start with: "Let's build the citation together. First — who created this record?"
2. After each answer, confirm and move to the next question
3. If they don't know an answer, help them figure it out from context clues in the document
4. After all five questions are answered, assemble the full citation
5. Show them the complete citation and explain how each part maps to the five questions

The goal is not just to produce a citation but to teach the researcher to think through the five questions automatically. After doing this 5-10 times, most people internalize the framework.

## Common Mistakes

- **Citing the website instead of the record**: "Ancestry.com" is where the source is held (Question 4), not what the source is (Question 2). The census itself is the source.
- **Missing "where in"**: "1870 census, Lancaster County" is incomplete. You need the township, page, dwelling, family, and line number.
- **Citing the index instead of the record**: If you looked at an index to find a record, cite the actual record, not the index (unless the index is all that survives).
- **Skipping the informant**: For vital records, the informant (who provided the information) matters for evaluating reliability. Note it when visible.
- **Not recording access dates**: Online sources change. Always include when you accessed a digital source.
