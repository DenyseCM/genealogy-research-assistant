# Search Planning by Era

Record availability varies dramatically by time period. Use this guide to determine which records to prioritize for a reasonably exhaustive search.

## Colonial Era (Pre-1776)

**Available records (prioritized):**
1. Church records (baptisms, marriages, burials) — often the primary source
2. Land records (warrants, patents, surveys, deeds)
3. Probate records (wills, inventories, administrations)
4. Tax lists (vary by colony)
5. Court records (county and colonial courts)
6. Ship passenger lists (many incomplete or lost)
7. Published compiled sources (colonial genealogies, county histories)

**Typically NOT available:**
- Civil vital records (not yet required)
- Census records (first U.S. census: 1790)
- Standardized military records (some militia records exist)

**Key repositories:** State archives, historical societies, FamilySearch, published colonial records series

## Revolutionary Era (1765–1790)

**Additional records beyond colonial era:**
1. Militia muster rolls and service records
2. Pension applications (filed later, often 1818–1850s)
3. Loyalty oath records (Pennsylvania: 1777–1778)
4. Confiscated property records (Loyalists)
5. Continental Army records
6. State constitutions and early government records

**Key insight:** Cross-reference loyalty oaths against militia rolls, tax lists, and church membership to understand an ancestor's wartime position.

## Early Republic (1790–1850)

**Available records:**
1. Federal census (1790–1840 name head of household only; 1850 names all household members)
2. Land records (federal land states: NARA; state land states: state archives)
3. Church records
4. Probate records
5. Tax records (increasingly standardized)
6. Naturalization records (from 1790 onward, but minimal detail before 1906)
7. War of 1812 and Mexican War military records
8. Early city directories
9. County histories (published 1880s–1890s, covering this period retrospectively)

**Key shift:** 1850 census is the first to name every person in the household. Research strategy changes dramatically at this boundary.

## Civil War Era (1850–1870)

**Additional records:**
1. Civil War military service records (Union and Confederate)
2. Civil War pension files (extremely detailed)
3. Draft registration records
4. Freedmen's Bureau records (formerly enslaved individuals)
5. Southern claims commission records
6. Homestead Act land records (from 1862)

**Key insight:** Civil War pension files are among the richest genealogical sources in existence. A widow's pension application may contain decades of family history.

## Gilded Age through Progressive Era (1870–1920)

**Golden age of documentation:**
1. Federal census (1870–1920, increasingly detailed)
2. State vital records begin (varies by state)
3. Immigration records (passenger lists become more detailed)
4. Naturalization records (post-1906: photographs, detailed biographical information)
5. City directories (annual, by occupation and address)
6. Newspaper coverage expands dramatically
7. School records
8. Employment records
9. Fraternal organization records
10. Social Security applications (SS-5, from 1936 but covering people born in this era)

## Modern Era (1920–1950)

**Most record types available:**
- All of the above, plus:
1. 1930 and 1940 census (1950 released 2022)
2. World War I and II draft registrations
3. Social Security records
4. More complete vital records
5. Hospital records
6. Expanded newspaper coverage
7. Photograph collections

**Key limitation:** Privacy laws restrict access to more recent vital records (typically 50–100 years depending on state and record type).

## Research Planning Template

For any research question, work through this checklist:

1. **Define the question**: What specific fact am I trying to establish?
2. **Identify the era**: What time period does this question cover?
3. **List available record types**: Using the era guide above, what records could exist?
4. **Prioritize**: Which records are most likely to contain the answer?
5. **Locate repositories**: Where are these records held?
6. **Search systematically**: Work through the list, documenting what was found and not found
7. **Evaluate exhaustiveness**: Have all reasonable sources been checked?
