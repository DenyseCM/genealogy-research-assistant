# Genealogical Record Types

Understanding what records exist, what they contain, and where to find them is essential for conducting a reasonably exhaustive search (GPS Element 1).

## Core Record Categories

### Vital Records (Birth, Marriage, Death)
- **What they contain**: Names, dates, places, parents' names, occupations, cause of death, informant identity
- **Why they matter**: Primary sources for life events; informant identity helps evaluate reliability
- **Availability varies**: U.S. vital registration began at different times by state (Pennsylvania: births/deaths from 1906, marriages vary by county)
- **Key detail**: Always note the informant — a spouse reporting a birth date is secondary information; the mother is primary

### Census Records
- **What they contain**: Names, ages, birthplaces, occupations, household composition, property values, literacy, immigration year (varies by census year)
- **Why they matter**: Snapshot of the family every 10 years; tracks migration, household changes, economic status
- **U.S. federal censuses**: 1790–1950 (publicly available); each decade asks different questions
- **State and local censuses**: Many states conducted their own (e.g., New York 1855, 1865, 1875, etc.)
- **Key detail**: Compare the same person across multiple census years — discrepancies reveal which information is reliable

### Church Records
- **What they contain**: Baptisms, marriages, burials, confirmations, membership lists, church discipline records
- **Why they matter**: Often predate civil vital records; may be the only record of events before government registration
- **Where to find**: Some remain with congregations; many microfilmed by FamilySearch; some at denominational archives
- **Key detail**: Denomination matters — Quaker meeting minutes are very different from Catholic parish registers

### Land and Property Records
- **What they contain**: Deeds, mortgages, land grants, warrants, surveys, tax records
- **Why they matter**: Prove residence, establish relationships (father to son transfers), track economic status, establish timeline
- **Where to find**: County recorder of deeds; state land offices; NARA for federal land states
- **Key detail**: Deed transfers between family members often reveal relationships not stated elsewhere

### Probate Records
- **What they contain**: Wills, administrations, inventories, accountings, guardianships, orphans' court records
- **Why they matter**: Name heirs and their relationships; reveal economic status; identify previously unknown children or spouses
- **Where to find**: County register of wills or probate court; state archives for older records
- **Key detail**: The complete probate file (not just the will) often spans multiple documents over years

### Military Records
- **What they contain**: Service records, pension applications, draft registrations, muster rolls, bounty land applications
- **Why they matter**: Pension files are often the richest genealogical source — they contain testimony, family details, medical history, residence information
- **Where to find**: NARA (federal); state archives (state militia/guard records)
- **Key detail**: Widow's pension applications often contain more family detail than the veteran's own file

### Newspaper Records
- **What they contain**: Obituaries, marriage announcements, legal notices, social columns, court proceedings, business news
- **Why they matter**: Provide context, social connections, and details not found in official records
- **Where to find**: Library of Congress Chronicling America; newspaper archives (Newspapers.com, GenealogyBank); local library collections
- **Key detail**: Check multiple papers — different papers covered different communities in the same area

### Immigration and Naturalization Records
- **What they contain**: Passenger lists, naturalization petitions, declarations of intention, alien registration
- **Why they matter**: Establish origin, arrival date, family traveling together, sponsoring relatives
- **Where to find**: NARA; Ancestry; FamilySearch; county courts (naturalization)
- **Key detail**: Naturalization records from 1906 onward contain significantly more detail than earlier records

### Court Records
- **What they contain**: Civil suits, criminal cases, divorce proceedings, name changes, adoptions, guardianships
- **Why they matter**: Reveal conflicts, relationships, property disputes, and life events not recorded elsewhere
- **Where to find**: County courthouse; state archives for older records
- **Key detail**: Divorce records often contain detailed testimony about family circumstances

### Tax Records
- **What they contain**: Property assessments, personal property, occupation, residence
- **Why they matter**: Annual records that track presence, property ownership, and economic status year by year
- **Where to find**: County tax offices; state archives; some microfilmed by FamilySearch
- **Key detail**: Disappearance from tax rolls can indicate death, migration, or economic change

## Planning a Reasonably Exhaustive Search

When helping a researcher plan their search:

1. Identify the time period and location
2. Determine which record types exist for that place and time
3. Prioritize by likely information yield
4. Note which repositories hold the records
5. Track what has been searched and what remains
6. Document negative searches (looked but not found) — these matter for GPS

See `references/search-planning-by-era.md` for era-specific guidance on which records to prioritize.
